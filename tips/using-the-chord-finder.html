6. Getting the Most Out of the Chord Finder

The Chord Finder tab works in reverse compared to the dictionary: instead of picking a chord name and getting a shape, you build a shape and the tool tells you what it's called. That makes it one of the most useful tools on the site for real-world playing situations, where you're the one holding an unfamiliar shape and need to know what it is.

  • Click any fret in the Chord Finder tab to mark a note — the tool identifies the closest matching chord as you go, updating live with every click.
  • Click a marked note again, or click its open-string indicator, to toggle it off.
  • Use Reset Frets to clear the fretboard and start over.
  • Use the Play button to hear exactly what you've clicked before you commit it to memory.

Behind the scenes, the tool works by collecting every note you've marked, figuring out which one is lowest in pitch (your bass note), and comparing the full set of notes against every chord formula it knows — favoring matches where your bass note lines up with the chord's root, since that's how real chords are voiced most of the time. If your shape doesn't perfectly match a standard chord, it will tell you honestly rather than force a bad guess, which is exactly what you want from a lookup tool.

It's a great way to reverse-engineer a chord you heard in a song, check what an unusual shape you found by ear is actually called, or double-check your own playing while learning a new song from a tab that might have a typo in it. Keep in mind the tool assumes standard EADGBE tuning — if you're playing in an alternate tuning like Drop D or DADGAD, the notes it detects won't match what you expect, since it has no way of knowing your strings are tuned differently.
