10. Diatonic Chords: Building Progressions from a Scale

Every major scale naturally produces seven chords — one built on each scale degree — and their qualities always follow the same pattern: I (major), ii (minor), iii (minor), IV (major), V (major), vi (minor), vii° (diminished).

  • In the key of C major, that's: C, Dm, Em, F, G, Am, and Bdim.
  • Any combination of these chords will "belong" to the key and sound harmonically coherent — it's the theory behind why progressions like I–V–vi–IV work in every key.
  • Try building your own progression using only diatonic chords from a key you like, then look each one up in the dictionary to find a comfortable voicing.

Minor keys have their own diatonic set, built the same way but starting from a minor scale: i (minor), ii° (diminished), III (major), iv (minor), v (minor), VI (major), VII (major) in natural minor — though in practice, the V chord is very often borrowed as major (raising its 3rd) to create a stronger pull back to the tonic, which is why so much minor-key music technically isn't purely diatonic.

This is also why the same handful of progression "shapes" (I–IV–V, vi–IV–I–V, ii–V–I) can be transplanted into any key without sounding wrong — the underlying scale-degree pattern is what actually matters, not the specific letter names. Once a progression is diatonic, every chord shares several notes with its neighbors, which is part of why diatonic progressions feel so stable and predictable compared to progressions that borrow chords from outside the key.

Understanding this pattern also makes it much easier to guess the next chord in a song you're learning by ear — if you know the key, you already know the seven most likely candidates before you've even heard the next chord change.
