14. Extended Chords in Pop & R&B: 9ths, 11ths, and 13ths

Extended chords stack notes beyond the basic 7th — a 9th is the 2nd an octave up, an 11th is the 4th an octave up, and a 13th is the 6th an octave up. Structurally they're just taller versions of the same 7th chords you already know, built by continuing to stack notes a third apart.

  • They add color and complexity without necessarily changing the chord's basic function — a Cmaj9 can often substitute directly for a plain C in a progression.
  • Common in R&B, neo-soul, and jazz-influenced pop for a lusher, more sophisticated sound.
  • Look up any extended quality (9, 11, 13, maj9, m9, and more) in the Chord Dictionary to see real, playable guitar voicings instead of just the theory.

On guitar, full extended chords are often "compressed" — a few of the middle notes get dropped to keep the shape playable with four fingers across six strings, while the notes that give the chord its characteristic color (the 9th, 11th, or 13th itself, plus the 3rd and 7th that define its quality) are kept. The 5th is usually the first note to go, since it adds the least color and is easiest to omit without losing the chord's identity. That's exactly what the voicings in the dictionary are built to show you — practical, real shapes rather than an unplayable full theoretical stack.

A classic jazz ii–V–I in C — Dm9, G13, Cmaj9 — is a good example of how these chords work in context: each one is a richer version of a simple ii–V–I (Dm, G, C), and swapping in the extended versions instantly gives the same progression a smoother, more "produced" quality without changing its underlying harmonic function at all.
