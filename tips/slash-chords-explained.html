4. Slash Chords Explained

A chord written as X/Y means "play chord X, but put note Y in the bass." It looks intimidating the first time you see it in a chart, but it's really just a bass note instruction layered on top of a chord you probably already know how to play.

  • If Y is already part of chord X's triad (like C/E or C/G), it's technically an inversion — the same chord, just with a different note on the bottom. Inversions are great for smooth, walking bass lines that connect chords stepwise instead of jumping around.
  • If Y isn't part of the triad (like Am/G, where G isn't in an A minor triad), it usually implies an extended chord — Am/G is really just Am7 spelled a different way — or it's simply a deliberate color choice by the arranger, sometimes called a hybrid or "polychord" voicing.
  • Try the slash chords listed under any chord in the Chord Dictionary tab to hear how a moving bass note changes the feel of a chord you already know.

Here's a concrete example of why this matters: a plain C–Am–F progression has bass notes that jump around (C, then A, then F). Rewrite the middle chord as G/B instead and swap the order to C–G/B–Am, and the bass line becomes C–B–A — a smooth, stepwise descent instead of a leap. That small change is often the difference between a progression that feels static and one that feels like it's "going somewhere," and it's exactly the kind of substitution songwriters reach for constantly.

Slash chords show up constantly in real songs specifically because a moving bass line under a static or slowly-changing set of chords creates forward motion — it's one of the cheapest, most effective tricks in songwriting. Piano ballads lean on this heavily, but it translates directly to guitar once you know which fretboard shape puts the right note on the bottom string.

One practical note: on guitar, playing a true slash chord sometimes means muting or skipping the string that would normally carry the root, since your lowest played string becomes the new bass note. The Chord Dictionary's slash chord voicings already account for this, so the fingering you see is playable as written, not a theoretical simplification.
