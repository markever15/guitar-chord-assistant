7. Capo Basics: How and Why to Use One

A capo clamps across all six strings at a fret, shortening the effective scale length and raising the pitch of every open string by that many semitones. That lets you keep playing the open-chord shapes you already know while sounding in a completely different key, which is exactly why it's one of the first accessories most guitarists buy.

  • Great for singers — keep familiar, easy shapes but shift the key to fit your vocal range without relearning a single chord.
  • Two guitarists can play the same song with different capo positions and different shapes, and it will still sound in tune together — a common trick in acoustic arrangements to create a fuller, chiming sound from two guitars.
  • To find the actual sounding chord, count up from the shape's letter name by the capo's fret number — a "C shape" with a capo on the 3rd fret sounds like Eb.

Here's a quick reference for a "C shape" moved up the neck with a capo: capo 1 sounds like C#, capo 2 like D, capo 3 like Eb, capo 4 like E, capo 5 like F, and so on — the same twelve-note cycle used throughout the Chord Dictionary. The shape in your hands never changes; only the capo's position determines what note actually comes out.

Beyond convenience, a capo also changes the guitar's tone. Fretted notes near the capo ring slightly brighter and tighter than the same pitch played further up the neck, because the vibrating string length is shorter. That's part of why capoed acoustic parts have a distinctive "chime" that's hard to get by simply playing a barre chord in the same key.

A capo isn't a crutch — some of the most iconic acoustic recordings ever made rely on one, precisely because open-string shapes ring differently than the barre chords you'd otherwise need to play the same key. If you don't own one yet, a basic clamp-style capo is inexpensive and works for the vast majority of songs you'll want to play.
